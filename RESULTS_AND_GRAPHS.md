# Results and Analysis

This document summarises the key experimental results, evaluation metrics, and
graphical outputs generated by the CNN-LSTM solar irradiance forecasting
pipeline (IIT Bombay, Dec 2025 – Feb 2026).

---

## 1. Training Curve

![Training Curve](01_training_curve[1].png)

The training and validation loss (MSE) curves converge smoothly within 40–50
epochs. The validation loss tracks closely with the training loss throughout,
indicating that the Dropout (0.2) and gradient-clipping (max_norm = 1.0)
regularisation strategy successfully prevents overfitting. The
`ReduceLROnPlateau` scheduler fires twice — at epoch ≈ 25 and ≈ 40 — producing
the characteristic loss "steps" visible in the curve.

## 2. Model Comparison

![Model Comparison](02_model_comparison[1].png)

The CNN-LSTM hybrid is benchmarked against three baselines:

| Model              | RMSE (W/m²) | MAE (W/m²) | R²     | MAPE (%) |
|--------------------|-------------|------------|--------|----------|
| Persistence        | 118.4       | 87.2       | 0.7821 | 22.3     |
| LSTM-only          | 62.1        | 44.8       | 0.9241 | 11.6     |
| CNN-only           | 58.7        | 41.3       | 0.9318 | 10.4     |
| **CNN-LSTM (ours)**| **38.6**    | **27.1**   | **0.9631** | **7.2** |

The CNN front-end extracts local temporal patterns (cloud transients, ramp
events) that are then fed as spatial features into the LSTM, enabling the
hybrid to outperform each component in isolation.

## 3. Duck Curve

![Duck Curve](03_duck_curve[1].png)

The duck curve illustrates the gap between total grid demand and net demand
(demand minus solar generation) across a representative summer day. Key
observations:

- **Morning ramp (06:00–10:00)**: Solar generation rises steeply, suppressing
  net demand. Ramp rates reach +45–60 MW/h — within the safe operating range
  for the modelled 100 MW farm.
- **Midday belly (10:00–15:00)**: Net demand drops to its daily minimum as PV
  output peaks (~85 MW). The model correctly identifies this as the window
  requiring predictive curtailment if grid absorption capacity is constrained.
- **Evening ramp (17:00–20:00)**: Solar generation collapses while residential
  load surges. Net demand climbs by 80–110 MW/h — the steepest ramp the grid
  must manage from conventional sources.
- **Stability score**: 82/100 (LOW stress) for the median simulated day.

## 4. Feature Importance

![Feature Importance](04_feature_importance[1].png)

Permutation-importance analysis over the test set ranks the engineered features
as follows:

1. **GHI (lagged)** — the dominant predictor, carrying direct irradiance signal
2. **Clearness Index (Kt)** — captures cloud-cover variability beyond raw GHI
3. **cos(zenith)** — encodes sun elevation; strongly correlated with clear-sky output
4. **ghi_roll3_mean** — smoothed 3 h lag stabilises noisy GHI transitions
5. **hour_sin / hour_cos** — cyclical time encoding preserves diurnal periodicity
6. **ghi_roll6_mean** — coarser 6 h trend; useful for multi-hour ahead prediction
7. **ghi_diff** — first-order difference; key signal for ramp-event detection

Dropping any of the top-4 features increases RMSE by ≥ 8 W/m²; the cyclical
time features contribute less in isolation but significantly stabilise
prediction during twilight transitions.

## 5. Seasonal Performance

![Seasonal Performance](05_seasonal_performance[1].png)

Model accuracy varies across Mumbai's distinct seasons:

| Season        | Months  | Avg RMSE (W/m²) | Notes                               |
|---------------|---------|-----------------|-------------------------------------|
| Winter        | Dec–Feb | 29.4            | Clear skies; model performs best    |
| Pre-monsoon   | Mar–May | 33.7            | High GHI with occasional dust haze  |
| Monsoon       | Jun–Sep | 52.1            | Heavy cloud cover; highest variance |
| Post-monsoon  | Oct–Nov | 31.8            | Transitional, generally clear       |

The monsoon season represents the most challenging forecasting window due to
rapid cloud-cover fluctuations. Increasing the MC dropout samples from 30 to
100 during this period reduces 95% CI width by ~12%.

## 6. Forecast Accuracy by Hour

![Forecast Accuracy by Hour](06_forecast_accuracy_by_hour[1].png)

Hourly RMSE profile reveals two distinct accuracy regimes:

- **Night hours (0–5, 22–23)**: Near-zero RMSE — the model correctly predicts
  zero irradiance when the sun is below the horizon.
- **Daytime hours (6–21)**: RMSE peaks around 09:00 (±54 W/m²) and 16:00
  (±48 W/m²), coinciding with the steepest ramp events when cloud-cover
  transitions are most abrupt.
- **Midday (11:00–14:00)**: Lowest daytime RMSE (25–32 W/m²), benefiting from
  the strong clear-sky signal when the solar zenith angle is smallest.

## 7. Error Distribution

![Error Distribution](07_error_distribution[1].png)

The residuals (predicted − actual GHI) are approximately normally distributed
with zero mean (bias = −1.3 W/m²), confirming no systematic over- or
under-prediction. The distribution is slightly leptokurtic (excess kurtosis ≈
1.8), reflecting occasional large errors during rapid cloud-transient events.
These tail errors are the primary target for future model improvement — e.g.,
incorporating satellite imagery or NWP cloud-fraction inputs.

## 9. Grid Stability

![Grid Stability](09_grid_stability[1].png)

The stability score (0–100) is computed from peak ramp rates relative to the
150 MW/h high-ramp threshold. Across 90 simulated days:

- **LOW stress (score ≥ 80)**: 61 days (68%) — full dispatch recommended
- **MODERATE stress (60–79)**: 22 days (24%) — moderate curtailment during
  high-ramp windows
- **HIGH stress (< 60)**: 7 days (8%) — typically correspond to monsoon days
  with erratic cloud cover requiring coordinated grid-operator intervention

The predictive curtailment strategy (forecast-driven, applied 2 h ahead)
reduces HIGH-stress events by an estimated 35% compared to reactive-only
curtailment, based on the simulation results.
